Issues

The Broadway Journal was published from January 4, 1845 - January 3, 1846. A 16-page issue was published every Saturday. Charles Frederick Briggs (founder and editor) and John Bisco (publisher) started the journal. Poe contributed from the start, began editing the journal in February 1845, became sole editor in July, and sole owner just a few months later in October.


November 15, 1845
Issue Preview

This issue begins with "A New Mode of Collecting a Library," ascribed to Poe by Burton Pollin. Poe responds to recent criticism of The Knickerbocker Magazine by transcribing the thoughts of a fictional, scatterbrained subscriber. The subscriber reflects on the journal's review of American authors, and he critiques their categorization of William Gilmore Simms as a "naughty man," instead finding him to be a "frank, manly Southron"-an assessment verified by the fact that Simms' work is published by Harpers (283). We also find here Sarah J. Hale's poem "The American Pioneer," which seems to be an early articulation of Manifest Destiny. Seeing these pioneers' movement west as evidence of providential design, she writes, "The Starry Banner o'er them, what foe will dare molest?/…/And so, in faith, in hope, and love, those pioneers move on,/ As though an Angel led the way toward the setting sun" (284). Attention to America and its legacy is also seen in the review of C. Edwards Lester's The Artist, the Merchant, and the Statesman. The editors particularly appreciate Lester's thoughts on what type of monument would best represent George Washington-the "Father of his Country… [whose] fame we well know can never die" (287). Two other pieces-R. S. Rowley's curious "Fancies" (284-85) and O. H. Mildeberger's poem "The Autumnal Leaf"-reflect on themes of time and mortality. - Andrew Johnson
