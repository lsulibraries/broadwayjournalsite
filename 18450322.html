Issues

The Broadway Journal was published from January 4, 1845 - January 3, 1846. A 16-page issue was published every Saturday. Charles Frederick Briggs (founder and editor) and John Bisco (publisher) started the journal. Poe contributed from the start, began editing the journal in February 1845, became sole editor in July, and sole owner just a few months later in October.


March 22, 1845
Issue Preview
Here, we get the third chapter of "The Voluminous History of the Little Longfellow War" (178-82). Poe blasts Outis for his claim that "The Raven" was a product of imitation and refutes Outis's charges. He then further bolsters his plagiarism accusation levelled at Longfellow. This issue also contains Poe's now famous article about "National Nomenclature." In it, he supports Washington Irving's proposed name "Appalachia" for the following reasons: "first, because it is distinctive; […] secondly, because it is indigenous, springing from the country itself, […] thirdly, because in employing it, we do honor to the Aborigines, whom, hitherto, we have at all points unmercifully despoiled, assassinated, and dishonored-fourthly, because it is musical, […] fifthly and lastly, because it is the suggestion of the most deservedly eminent among all the pioneers of American literature" (187). The editors criticize the "New York Gallery of the Fine Arts" for its inclusion of copies of European art, contending familiarly that "a collection of Original American works would have a beneficial effect… and lead to self-dependence in other thing of seemingly greater importance" (187). The editors also, in a review of the latest "The American Review," explain that their "politics… being purely personal, rather than considerations of principle," can never be the "pervading feature" of their magazine (183). Finally, we find a strange apology for Brigg's review of Fuller's Woman in the Nineteenth Century, in which the author largely reaffirms his conservative ideas of woman's role as a mother and a wife (182-83). - Caleb Doan
